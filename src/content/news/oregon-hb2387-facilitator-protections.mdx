Oregon's regulated psilocybin services program — established under 2020's Measure 109 — has a new law affecting facilitators as of 1 January 2026. HB 2387 allows licensed healthcare providers who are also registered psilocybin facilitators to deliver psilocybin services without risking discipline from their professional licensing board for doing so.

## Context: how Oregon's model differs from everywhere else in this directory

Oregon is structurally different from every other destination in this directory. Rather than a legal carve-out (Netherlands) or absence of prohibition (Jamaica) or protected traditional use (Peru), Oregon runs a fully state-licensed and regulated facilitator model: licensed service centres, licensed facilitators, and state oversight, open to adults 21+. Licensing began in January 2023, with centres opening from mid-2023.

As of early 2026, roughly 22–23 of the 35 originally licensed service centres remain open — some closures have been attributed to the program's $10,000 annual licence fee and associated compliance costs — with approximately 400 active facilitators and an estimated 15,000–16,000 clients served to date.

## Why this specific law matters

Before HB 2387, a licensed healthcare provider (for example, a nurse or therapist) who also worked as a psilocybin facilitator risked professional-board discipline for doing so, since psilocybin remains a Schedule I substance federally. The new law removes that specific risk at the state level for providers operating within Oregon's regulated program.
