Ayahuasca retreat tourism is most associated with Peru, but Australians researching this category will also encounter Costa Rica and, less commonly, Netherlands-based offerings. These three destinations differ substantially — legally, culturally, and practically.

## Peru: the clearest legal footing, no centre licensing

Peru has the strongest legal basis of the three, via a specific reservation to the 1971 UN Convention on Psychotropic Substances and a 2008 cultural-heritage declaration protecting traditional ayahuasca ceremony. This reflects Peru's long, continuous tradition of ceremonial ayahuasca use, particularly among Amazonian Indigenous groups including the Shipibo-Conibo.

What this legal clarity does *not* provide is a licensing or accreditation system for retreat centres — Peru has none, as of 2026. The two Peru centres we've reviewed for this directory illustrate the range this creates in practice: one publishes an unusually detailed, ICEERS-developed screening process and a specific, multi-step emergency evacuation plan; the other's screening rigor has been described inconsistently in third-party accounts. Same country, same broad legal position, meaningfully different operator practices — which is the whole reason this site's vetting checklist exists.

## Costa Rica: legally ambiguous, not clearly legal

This is a genuinely different legal position from Peru's, not just a variation on the same theme. Ayahuasca isn't specifically named in Costa Rican law, but DMT (its active compound) is controlled — and Costa Rica's Ministry of Health issued a specific public health warning covering ayahuasca and ibogaine use in January 2025. Retreats operate there without routine prosecution, but "not routinely prosecuted" is a materially weaker position than Peru's specific cultural-heritage protection. Treat Costa Rica as ambiguous rather than confirmed-legal.

## Netherlands: unclear for ayahuasca specifically

The Netherlands' well-known legal clarity is about psilocybin truffles, via a specific and well-documented carve-out — it doesn't automatically extend to ayahuasca, which involves a different controlled compound (DMT) entirely. We haven't researched a specific, sourced legal basis for ayahuasca ceremonies in the Netherlands for this guide, so we're deliberately not making a claim either way here. If you're considering a Netherlands-based ayahuasca offering, this is a specific question to put directly to the operator, with a request for their legal basis in writing.

## Practical differences beyond legality

Peru retreats are typically longer (commonly 7–12+ days) than a comparable psilocybin retreat, reflecting both the tradition (multiple ceremonies over consecutive nights is standard) and the practicalities of reaching the Amazon. Travel logistics from Australia are also more demanding for Peru than for Netherlands-based options — see the destination pages for typical routing and cost.
